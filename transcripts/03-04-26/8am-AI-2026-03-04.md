### Summary

David Olsson, Hanif Janmohamed, Juan, Ying, Bailey Klinger, and Jen Boger discussed Ying's recent debate performance, noting their improvisation and the pivot of the debate topic from AI domination to policies and data privacy. The main conversation shifted to autonomous agents, with David Olsson explaining the importance and evolution of projects like Open Claw and their own project "Emily" with Martin Robson, while Ying, Juan Rojas, Bailey Klinger, and Jen Boger expressed concerns about the rapid rate of change in AI, its impact on the education system, and the risk of skill degradation and loss of critical thinking among future generations. The participants ultimately agreed that critical thinking remains the core necessary skill, and universities must undergo structural changes to adapt to the advent of autonomous agents and the transactional and relational engagement with AI.

### Details

- **Initial Check-in and Concerns**: David Olsson and Hanif Janmohamed started the meeting with a brief personal check-in, noting tiredness and lack of sleep. Hanif Janmohamed mentioned they were potentially developing an allergy to dogs.
- **Ying's Recent Debate Performance**: David Olsson welcomed Ying, congratulating them on their recent debate in the UBC Okanagan debate series. David Olsson noted the audience style and engagement were good, and the language used was appropriate for the attendees.
- **Audience Engagement and Strategy in the Debate**: David Olsson explained that Ying had been assigned to the negative side of the debate topic but was ultimately on the positive side, and they used in-the-moment market research to gauge the audience. David Olsson praised Ying for their clever and practical approach to getting the audience to applaud five times early in the debate.
- **Improvisation and Performance Feedback**: Ying explained that they had to improvise by asking the audience to applaud because the stage was dark. They noted that the debate topic had pivoted from AI domination to discussing policies and data privacy, diverting from the intended focus on AI.
- **Discussion of the OpenClaw Project**: The conversation shifted to autonomous agents, with Ying asking if anyone was using Open Claw. David Olsson confirmed they had mentioned it in an email, noting that Open Claw has spawned many similar projects using the pattern of autonomous work.
- **Risks and Themes of Autonomous Agents**: Ying expressed concern about Open Claw, citing news about malfunctions, injected viruses, and the suggestion of running it on a computer without internet access. David Olsson explained that the news polarizes the topic toward fear, but encouraged Ying to focus on the themes and patterns of personal autonomous agents.
- **The Emily Autonomous Agent Project**: David Olsson shared that they are collaborating with Martin Robson on a project called "Emily," an autonomous agent intended to learn and grow with its user in a sandbox environment. Emily is currently being trained to understand oil and gas compliance in Alberta and is writing its own code.
- **Emily's Current Capabilities and Memory Structure**: Emily features a four-level memory system, including historical conversation storage, active context, and a set of memories for reasoning and thinking.
- **The Impending Inflection Point of Agency**: David Olsson emphasized that this year will see agency manifest in a significant way, representing a massive inflection point for knowledge workers.
- **Education System and the Rate of Change**: David Olsson and Ying discussed the challenges of the education system, with David Olsson stating that universities are the least prepared to teach in this new environment.
- **Critical Thinking as the Core Skill**: David Olsson stressed that the focus should be on critical thinking and "how you know," rather than simply what you know. Ying worried that launching autonomous agents will eliminate the opportunity for younger people to struggle and develop concrete skills.
- **Managing Risk in Agent Swarms**: David Olsson countered the idea that there will be "no ladder to climb," asserting that peers who use swarm agents are constantly managing risk, construction, and validation.
- **The Future Role of Universities**: Ying questioned the relevance of universities if knowledge becomes a commodity. Juan Rojas argued that autonomous agents remove busy work, freeing people for "better or higher work".
- **Shifting Roles and the Need for Context**: Juan Rojas suggested the future role might resemble a librarian, cataloging information and adding context for AI to create new value.
- **Concerns about Skill Degradation**: Ying reiterated their concern that a future generation will lack experiential knowledge to make high-level decisions. Bailey Klinger offered an analogy: just as people still exercise despite cars and machines existing, the purpose of education remains to build critical thinking skills.
- **Motivation and the Value of Humanities**: David Olsson used their degree in English as an example, stating that they pursued it because they wanted the knowledge.
- **Historical Analogy and Systemic Change**: Jen Boger provided the analogy of the car displacing the horse industry, suggesting that humans might stop coding except for enjoyment. Jen Boger emphasized that university systems must change.
- **Transactional vs. Relational AI Engagement**: Hanif Janmohamed suggested there is a significant difference between transactional and relational engagement with AI, pointing to a potential opportunity in exploring the relational aspect.
- **The Pace of Change and Loss of Resilience**: Ying expressed angst about the rapid pace of change, fearing future generations' cognitive thinking is at risk. David Olsson responded that the rate of change will be constant and that resilience is required.
- **Opportunities for Influence**: David Olsson reminded Ying that they are in a position to influence policy at a global level through UBC.
- **Multidisciplinary Approach to Critical Thinking**: David Olsson suggested that the method of how one thinks is "sideloaded" at university, often narrowly defined by discipline.
