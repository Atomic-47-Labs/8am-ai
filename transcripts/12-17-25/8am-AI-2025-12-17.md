# 📝 Notes

Dec 17, 2025

## 8am AI!

### Summary

The meeting, attended by David Olsson, Bailey Klinger, André Bertram, Fulvio Ciano, James Giroday, Jason Kryski, and Hanif Janmohamed, covered the shift in AI discussions from learning to high-level topics, emphasizing that AI's value increase is noticeable and its adoption is crucial for future opportunities, with David Olsson showcasing a joyful, innovative motion capture yoga application and Fulvio Ciano detailing the rapid advancements in multimodal AI for data extraction and content creation. Participants discussed the need for building systemic "skills" to solve the "problem of the problem" for AI integration, the challenges of AI adoption in small businesses and healthcare due to systemic and cultural issues, and the need for new consultancies to provide multidisciplinary deployment solutions. The conversation concluded by exploring AI as a "cultural technology" that can solve the productivity problem through better onboarding and its role in enabling abundance by drastically reducing costs, leading to a profound cultural shift in the definitions of productivity and work.

### Details

- **Introduction and Initial Discussions** The meeting started with David Olsson welcoming new participants, Bailey Klinger from Peru and André Bertram from Kelowna, and existing participants Fulvio Ciano and James Giroday. Olsson mentioned that they had been discussing AI, noting that while initially the conversations focused on learning together, they have shifted to higher-level topics. David Olsson introduced the perspective that AI's impact on productivity is difficult to quantify but its value increase is noticeable, especially on a personal level.

- **AI Plateauing and Value Increase** David Olsson discussed the idea of AI potentially plateauing, although their current experience suggests otherwise, with a focus on figuring out the value increase of AI adoption within their company and commercially. Olsson noted that the professional view involves identifying a "runway" for service in the market, but the more interesting aspects involve random, innovative applications. They also emphasized that if people do not learn to use AI, they may struggle to find opportunities, a perspective shared by a colleague teaching at UBCO.

- **Joyful and Innovative AI Use Cases** David Olsson shared a personal project demonstrating the innovative potential of AI: creating a motion capture (mocap) yoga application in the browser. Olsson detailed how they used a simple script and text-to-speech to develop an application that tracks stillness in poses to progress through a yoga session, highlighting this as a "joyful part" of working with AI outside of their rigorous professional projects. Olsson contrasted this personal exploration with their day job in quality control for oil and gas, expressing hope and admiration for the capabilities of tools like ChatGPT.

- **Scientific Data Extraction and Multimodal AI** Fulvio Ciano discussed their professional work with David Olsson on targeted ingestion of PDFs to extract scientific data from published papers and patents. Ciano noted that this required sophisticated processing of both images and text and creating a custom data structure. Ciano highlighted the rapid advancement of multimodal capabilities in state-of-the-art models, which has made it easier to generate diverse content for personal projects like a Christmas-themed D&D module, including images, maps, and narratives, without switching AI systems.

- **Multimodal Capabilities and Tool Usage** Fulvio Ciano elaborated that modern AI platforms like Chat GPT and Gemini are skilled at using multiple models and are proficient in multimodal processing, enabling the production of cohesive content like visuals, story, and music. Ciano mentioned that these models can effectively utilize folders of diverse content formats, including PDFs, JSONs, and CSVs (though they expressed an aversion to CSVs as anything other than an output format), in a multimodal Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) manner. Fulvio Ciano contrasted this with an earlier NFT project that required multiple specific models and tool environments, noting that the same complex tasks can now be done within one environment like Gemini with a single Multi-Competent Process (MCP).

- **Skills Development and Systemic AI Integration** Jason Kryski joined the discussion and brought up the concept of "Claude skills," which David Olsson had previously discussed, suggesting that there is value in building systems, or "skills," to solve the "problem of the problem" by documenting processes and creating a corpus of knowledge, strategy, and tactics. David Olsson agreed that this strategy of building skills is worth pursuing, especially since AI context windows have massively increased, mitigating previous limitations. Jason Kryski observed that highly skilled individuals are integrating AI into their workflows using environments like IDEs for things beyond software development.

- **The Evolution of Software and Vertical SaaS** Jason Kryski questioned the long-term viability of vertical SaaS (Software as a Service) as an interim stage, noting that the market is currently seeing a "slow roll" of AI application despite criticism about the lack of immediate value creation and automation. Fulvio Ciano agreed that current methods will change, suggesting a transition state is necessary to gain workflow insights, but ultimately predicting "a lot less software". Jason Kryski stressed that the path will be interrupted, and infrastructure built now is likely to be invalidated by future disruptions.

- **Documentation and the Consultant's Role** James Giroday inquired about documentation as an area of focus, which David Olsson supported as being the "most vital thing right now" for moving up the stack and leveraging IP through descriptions that can be integrated into various systems. David Olsson emphasized the need for a foundational place for thinking that has integrity, referring to the "how of how of how". Jason Kryski described the increasing demand for multidisciplinary AI consultancies that parachute in to assess a business, build tools, and retrain teams, suggesting that a one-stop solution is needed for deployment.

- **AI Adoption Challenges in Small Businesses and Healthcare** Bailey Klinger highlighted the challenges of AI adoption in small, non-technical local economies like Kelowna, which are dominated by businesses like contractors and doctors, noting that despite clear opportunities for productivity gains, these are not happening. Bailey Klinger provided a specific use case of a doctor needing AI to structure patient history intake, saving significant time, but the doctor lacked the technical knowledge to implement it. David Olsson confirmed having explored this exact healthcare use case two years prior but found systemic cultural problems within healthcare, where budgets and predetermined spending block innovation, even for seemingly easy ideas.

- **Sectors Ripe for AI Disruption** Fulvio Ciano countered by pointing to tech-adjacent sectors like video games and telco, which have seen enormous AI impact because they were already using technology for automation. Ciano noted that whole departments in the video game industry have dramatically shrunk due to AI tools, and customer service and call centers have seen huge impacts from chatbots and AI-driven process design. André Bertram, who works in healthcare software, echoed the issues of cultural approaches to innovation within the healthcare system and suggested the industry is currently focused on "selling shovels" (tools) rather than profoundly addressing underlying problems.

- **Change Management and Upskilling** Jason Kryski shared consulting experience showing that businesses are resistant to mass layoffs, even when AI could significantly reduce staffing needs, due to concerns about cultural damage. Jason Kryski suggested that adopting AI requires moving people around and upskilling them with "meta skills" to understand how to use AI strategically, noting that most people are currently stuck in the "chat layer," replacing search with AI. Bailey Klinger emphasized that while change management is a problem, the small, owner-run businesses common in the local economy could greatly benefit from simple, pieced-together tools to solve immediate problems like labor shortages.

- **Cultural Technology and Innovation** Hanif Janmohamed shifted the focus, expressing curiosity about seeing AI as a "cultural technology" beyond just a productivity tool, asking how it can be used inventively to do things currently impossible. David Olsson suggested that the productivity problem will likely be solved through addressing the cultural problem. Fulvio Ciano noted the historic reflexive relationship between cultural change and technology, suggesting this current cycle will be profound and will likely unravel the cultural-technical loop.

- **AI and Onboarding as a Cultural Solution** James Giroday questioned David Olsson on how the cultural aspect solves the productivity problem. David Olsson explained that the reflexive capacity of AI can better onboard people to the concept and practice of AI by offering a "concierge experience" that addresses individual problems and guides them through solutions, reducing threat and increasing comfort. David Olsson argued that focusing on systemic problems not currently addressed, often those reliant on paper, is a viable business strategy.

- **Layoffs, Abundance, and Cost Reduction** Bailey Klinger offered a perspective on layoffs as an enabler for broader productivity and culture creation, arguing that productivity inherently leads to fewer people doing more, freeing others for new ventures. Bailey Klinger gave an example of creating a free AI chatbot for entrepreneurs in developing countries, which would have been too costly without AI, demonstrating how the technology drastically reduces the cost of entry and enables niche projects. David Olsson clarified that the issue is where the layoffs occur, suggesting that enabling abundance through external contracts feels different than internal workforce reductions. André Bertram concluded that this curve will lead to a cultural shift in the definitions of productivity and work, allowing people who are laid off to leverage free AI tools to build unique applications and offer new services more cheaply and widely.

- **Relationship with AI and Future Models** David Olsson shared an example of a peer in the modular home industry using AI for their sales funnel by being "vulnerable enough to ask the AI how to do it". David Olsson highlighted the polarity between using AI for compute (as they and Fulvio Ciano do for quality control) and using it for character/companion models that build multi-dimensional memories, which is the focus of a peer's new model. Hanif Janmohamed agreed that both polarities must be addressed, but the tendency is toward productivity, while the "elephant in the room" is AI's potential as a cultural technology.
