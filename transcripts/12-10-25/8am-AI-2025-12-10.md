## 8am AI!

### Summary

David Olsson initiated a discussion on AI bias, detailing his team's efforts to mitigate bias in chemical informatics by treating it as pure compute and processing research papers, with Fulvio Ciano currently working on data quality validation. Jason Kryski and David Olsson discussed the changing dynamics of startups, emphasizing the shift from proximity to capital to solving "burning problems" with AI, resulting in verticalized solutions, while Ying suggested using their students to build specialized AI tools for academic challenges, and Spencer MacBeth criticized traditional accelerator models as "incredibly out of touch." David Olsson, Jason Kryski, Jen Boger, and Ying also debated strategies for promoting AI adoption and overcoming institutional inertia in large organizations and universities, with David Olsson and Ying planning a follow-up coffee meeting to discuss successful curriculum and industry integration models.

### Details

- **Initial Discussion on AI and Bias** David Olsson began the meeting by sharing a visual representation of Emily, an AI creating its memories, which involved a graph illustrating relationships and biases in defining concepts like "joyful". He noted that the value of the AI system, from his perspective, is based on the bias built into it. The speaker questioned the reception of AI tools, suggesting that users often experience them without questioning the underlying bias, and that reflective output from AI often simply confirms existing input.

- **Bias in Systems and Chemical Informatics** David Olsson elaborated that the work of his team involves trying to "beat the bias out of these systems" by treating it as pure compute, analyzing research papers for chemical structures in the cheminformatics industry. They are focused on making data functional, which contrasts with working with information, as they believe data has less bias than information. He mentioned that the work involves science and engineering levels, concentrating on knowledge and reasoning rather than emotional impact, even though related discussions later in the week may be emotionally based.

- **Processing Research Papers and AI Applications** David Olsson explained that they are processing scientific research papers focused on chemical structures, particularly for clients involved in patenting these structures. Ying noted the challenges in the academic space, including the need for agents to automate repetitive tasks like downloading PDFs, which David Olsson confirmed is possible through building specialized tools rather than general chatbots. Fulvio Ciano is currently working on the output, generating reports to validate the quality of data received from these reports.

- **Student Involvement in AI Tool Building** Jason Kryski suggested that Ying should utilize their "AI for business kids" (students) to build the tools needed, viewing the students as an "army" or "minions" to solve problems. Ying replied that their students already build their own startups, and that they believe if they figure out solutions to common academic problems, the entire academic community could benefit. Jason Kryski related this to a similar situation with a young guy interested in AI automation for local businesses, emphasizing that individual priorities differ from those of a team or students.

- **Shifting Dynamics of Startups and Capital** Jason Kryski discussed how the dynamics around startups have changed, suggesting that success is no longer about proximity to capital, but rather proximity to problems or industry. The value has shifted, with capital becoming more commoditized as it follows what becomes obvious, rendering advice from two years ago potentially outdated. Ying expressed appreciation for the focus on problems over finance, mentioning a podcast about AI self-financing, where AI agents solve problems and analyze investment spaces for high ROI.

- **AI in Finance and Business Strategy** David Olsson supported the idea of AI being a steward for a company, managing finances and ensuring appropriate allocation. He noted that while generating money with algorithms is difficult, managing existing money is "fairly easy to do". He also emphasized the importance of dynamic views on business, stating that success requires being as close as possible to the source of the problem, and that multi-year plans are no longer believable in the current fast-moving landscape.

- **Product Market Fit and Vertical Solutions** Jason Kryski stressed the importance of finding a "burning problem" that AI can code a solution for to meet a minimum requirement. He suggested a model where AI automation engineers work with companies to solve challenges and then productize those solutions into "verticalized solution[s]" or "vertical SaaS" for specific markets and industries. David Olsson agreed with the need to deal with the problem set of the community rather than starting with a widget idea, which used to be a valid path.

- **Need for Domain Experts and Business Analysts** David Olsson explained that the sweet spot for business now needs to be "tight and close," relying on domain experts who can quantify the ROI at the outset in industries like pharmacy, medical science, insurance, and construction. He is considering reshaping their business to include business analysts dedicated to solving problems, similar to how a student would focus on a problem over a semester (40 to 160 hours). He suggested that the pattern of software consumed, purchased, and sunsetting has been the norm for all enterprise software, and that AI is running into the same software adoption problems in fields like oil and gas.

- **Sales and Adoption Challenges in Large Organizations** Jason Kryski emphasized the need for "founder sales energy" and conviction to evangelize a solution through an organization to foster adoption. David Olsson confirmed that their industries require significant cultural change, and they are pursuing a high volume of sales calls (25 a week), comparing it to "boiler room numbers". Ying pointed out that for big corporations and institutions like universities, top leaders must be willing to adopt AI, referencing the success of B2B companies like Anthropic.

- **Gap in AI Adoption and Paperwork Automation** Ying lamented the lack of AI adoption at the university level, noting the immense amount of manual administrative work, such as processing receipts and grants, which even simple automation could address. David Olsson shared an example of a multi-million dollar AI company in pharmacy that struggles with basic tasks like processing a PDF, demonstrating that they are limited by their focus and manual processes. Fulvio Ciano added that these organizations are limited by their expertise, not the available technology.

- **Institutional Inertia and Research Value** David Olsson described the university environment as a "monolithic tectonic plate" that resists innovation in research processes, adhering to old-school communication and forms. Ying questioned the value of research and publishing when AI is advancing so rapidly, noting that except for the AI track, most academic conferences continue as if the world has not changed. David Olsson observed that the outside world is generally less urgent about the need to act compared to their group.

- **Outdated Startup Acceleration Models** Spencer MacBeth shared their experience with the Fundable program at Accelerate Okanagan, finding it "incredibly out of touch" with current trends, particularly regarding the speed at which vertical SaaS products can be built. They noted that the focus remained on traditional methods like data rooms and investor outreach, and that their own efforts required too much hands-on engagement, emphasizing the industry's demand for an "absolute end to end solution".

- **Importance of Storytelling and Sales in Startup Success** Ying advised Spencer MacBeth and their partner, Graham, that despite having a top-notch product, navigating human bias and heuristics is crucial, as the presentation's aesthetics and the story told still influence investor decisions. Ying suggested that they need a salesperson on their team to effectively resonate with investors and networks. David Olsson compared Accelerate Okanagan to Platform Calgary, suggesting that both accelerators have inertia and that institutions like UBCO are fundamentally behind, reinforcing that change is easier at the cohort level.

- **Building Industry Connections in Education** David Olsson recounted the successful interactive design program at Catalina College, which had 400 active relationships with companies, resulting in 95% student placement before graduation. The program was eventually cancelled due to a provincial political shift focusing on academic learning over applied learning. Ying expressed their intense effort to build such industry networks, recognizing it as the most valuable asset a university can possess for professional student placement.

- **Addressing AI Literacy and Organizational Change** Ying sought feedback for an upcoming workshop for C-suite and board directors on AI literacy and application, emphasizing the top-down nature of change and the need to educate non-tech-savvy leaders. Jen Boger supported this need, stating that the core problem is that business and education systems were built pre-AI, and decision-makers often lack current technological expertise. Jason Kryski proposed that the most effective approach for Ying's workshop would be to "terrify them with the fear" of missing out, as fear of loss is a stronger motivator than desire for gain.

- **Strategic Approach to Influence and Experimentation** Jason Kryski suggested that their group has a high level of leverage in the local community of Kelowna and an opportunity to create an AI-first model that could be exported. Jen Boger agreed on the necessity of operating in "experiment mode," where articulating assumptions and transparency are critical for objective decision-making. David Olsson advised Ying to get the leaders to "self-select" into action by making the conclusion so obvious that they cross the chasm themselves. Ying concluded that a balanced approach is needed, highlighting the potential risks of AI alongside the fear of missing out.

- **Strategy for Institutional Change and Board Engagement** David Olsson advised Ying on dealing with risk when advocating for change at the institutional level, stating that if risk is brought up, it must be addressed immediately to maintain control of the narrative. They suggested that Ying focus on the risk of students not being relevant to society and industry as the most important factor. David Olsson also recommended identifying three or four "champions" on the board who will self-select to be allies and continue conversations after initial presentations.

- **Curriculum Relevance and Industry Integration** Ying expressed the need to learn from David Olsson about successful models from their previous institution to discuss with their own leaders. David Olsson shared their experience of having an "emerging technologies" course that allowed the curriculum to be rewritten every year, which helped keep the program relevant and served as a loadstone for attracting new students. Ying agreed that while they have one course decided for "AI for business," other core courses were determined long ago and need updated examples, noting that consumers will soon use AI agents for shopping.

- **Leveraging Industry Connections for Student Success** Ying articulated their goal to increase student employment rates by establishing close connections with 400 companies in the region. David Olsson explained that bringing industry professionals in as part-time instructors was a significant benefit, as this provided their companies access to top students, which David Olsson called the advantage of involving industry. David Olsson emphasized that instructors' commitment in this setup is wholly to the students, as the pay for part-time university instruction is poor.

- **Model of Success for University Programs** David Olsson discussed Northeastern University's satellite campus in Vancouver as an example of a successful model, which integrates tightly with the community by hiring community leaders to teach and holding industry events. They noted that Northeastern, despite being in an over-serviced university market, is "killing it" by acting like an entrepreneurial business and focusing on the tech space. David Olsson suggested that this upstart model could be used by Ying when presenting to the board.

- **Next Steps and Collaboration** Ying expressed gratitude for being included in the discussion group and valuing the knowledge shared. Ying and David Olsson planned to schedule a coffee meeting, preferably next week, to continue their conversation, with David Olsson offering to explain on record how things worked at their previous institution.
