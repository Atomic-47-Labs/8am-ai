## The promotion nobody assigned

The fourth-largest thread in the corpus is about a single shift the group
kept living through and re-describing: the move from doing *atomic* work —
tasks, functions, units — to doing *architecture* — systems, orchestration,
the shape of the whole. Nobody was promoted into this. The tools pushed
everyone up the stack whether they were ready or not.

## The arc

- **2024 — projects as objects.** Early sessions are about *things you
  build*: MLM and blockchain experiments, specific apps, discrete projects.
  The unit of thought is the deliverable.
- **Late 2024 / early 2025 — design enters.** User experience and design
  become explicit topics. David builds a task-management tool. The question
  starts shifting from "what should this do" to "how should this be shaped."
- **Mid 2025 — planning at system scale.** The Alberta Energy Regulator
  project planning discussions, data-platform differences, the recognition
  that the hard decisions are architectural — which platform, which data
  flow — not which feature.
- **Late 2025 — ecosystem awareness.** Cohere's position in the Canadian AI
  ecosystem; thinking about where you sit in a larger system, not just what
  you ship into it.
- **2026 — the shift named.** "Slop code" culture forces the question: if the
  code is cheap and disposable, what's *not* cheap? The answer the group
  converges on: the architecture, the control plane, the system thinking. By
  spring 2026 it's explicit — the move "to system thinking and orchestration"
  as the actual job.

## What "atomic to architecture" actually means

The group's clearest version of the idea: developers were trained to do
*atomic* work — small, well-defined tasks, done well. LLMs are excellent at
exactly that atomic layer. So the human contribution has to move to where the
machine is weak: deciding what system to build, how the pieces fit, which
decision-gates matter, what "good" even means here.

This reframes a lot of the corpus's anxiety. The fear that "AI will do my
job" is really "AI will do the *atomic* part of my job" — which was never the
valuable part, just the visible one. The uncomfortable corollary: people who
only ever did atomic work, and never built the muscle for architecture, have
the most to lose and the least practice at what's left.

## The through-line with everything else

This thread is the spine the others hang on:

- **Agentic work** is atomic-to-architecture applied to *who does the task*
  (you direct, the swarm executes).
- **Context engineering** is it applied to *what the model gets* (you design
  the corpus, the model fills it in).
- **Education** is it applied to *what to teach* (the meta-skill, not the
  task).

Every major thread in the corpus is, underneath, a version of the same
promotion: stop doing the unit, start designing the system that does the
units.

## What stayed true

1. **The atomic layer commoditized; the architectural layer didn't.** Two
   years of evidence: the closer your work was to a discrete task, the faster
   it was absorbed. The closer it was to system design, the more it held.
2. **Architecture is a muscle, not a title.** The people who adapted were the
   ones who'd already practiced thinking in systems — regardless of seniority.
3. **"What's not cheap?" is the right question.** When code goes to near-zero
   cost, value relocates to whatever code can't supply: judgment, structure,
   knowing what's worth building. The group kept finding the value by asking
   what *didn't* get cheaper.

## Open question

If everyone is pushed up into architecture, who keeps the architects honest?
Atomic work had built-in feedback — it compiled or it didn't. System
thinking fails slowly and quietly. The corpus has the question — "how do you
know" — but applied to architecture, the answer is even harder, because the
errors don't surface for months.
