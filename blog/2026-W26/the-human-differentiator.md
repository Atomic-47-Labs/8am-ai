## Two conversations, four weeks apart

In late May, Fulvio and David spent a meeting walking through the future intersection
of human and LLM thought-space. Neuralink as a worrying proposition not because of
the hardware but because of the software alignment that would follow. Ozempic as a
metaphor: a drug becomes acceptable not when the science changes but when enough
visible people normalize it. The conclusion was uncomfortable: people will adopt
neural augmentation faster than they think they will, for the same reason people
adopted telephones — once you have it, not having it is a deficit.

A week later, David and Ying landed on the inverse claim. As the world advances
digitally, human-to-human connection becomes *more* valuable, not less. The core of
education, they noted, isn't transmission of information — the model can do that.
It's socialization. It's learning how to be a person around other people. The thing
the model cannot give you is the experience of being seen by another human.

These two claims sit awkwardly next to each other. Both feel true. The thread the
group keeps pulling on is the seam between them.

## What changes when the models get sharper

Most of the conversation around AI assumes the relevant variable is capability.
What happens when the model can do X? The group's working hypothesis is that
capability is mostly a solved problem now — the actual variable is what humans
choose to do *with* the time and attention the model frees up.

Two paths visible from the conversations:

1. **Compression.** Use the model to do more of the same. Fill the freed time with
   more output, more tasks, more meetings. This is what most companies are doing.
   It's not obviously bad but it doesn't differentiate anything.

2. **Reallocation.** Use the model to take over the parts of work that were never
   the point — the atomic execution, the boilerplate, the lookup — and spend the
   freed attention on the parts that were always the point. Substantive
   conversation. Mentorship. Going deep on a problem with another human in the
   room. Building something only this combination of people could build.

## The hiring problem is a symptom

The group keeps returning to the difficulty of hiring well. Candidates can run an
LLM as a co-pilot during interview. Coding tests stop testing what they used to
test. The standard response is to make the tests harder or weirder. The more
interesting response, surfacing in several recent meetings, is to stop trying to
filter for capability and start filtering for the things the model can't fake:
genuine curiosity, the ability to disagree well, the experience of having built
something with other humans and survived the friction.

The baseline interviews in *Blade Runner 2049* aren't testing knowledge. They're
testing whether something is on the other end at all. That's the shift.

## What gets more valuable

The group's implicit answer, never stated this directly but visible across the
thread:

- **Place.** Living on a farm. Being somewhere specific with specific people.
  Cultivating the parts of life that can't be done over a screen.
- **Long-arc relationships.** Fulvio's metaphor about his adopted daughter — there's
  no hardware relationship, but having been her dad since she was seven, the
  software is aligned. That kind of alignment takes years and can't be sped up.
- **Time spent in the room.** The meetings themselves. The fact that this group
  has met every Wednesday for nine months is the actual artifact. Everything
  written here is downstream of that.

## Open thread

If the model can do most of what we used to call "thinking work," and the human
contribution shifts to the things the model can't simulate, what does that mean
for the structure of work? The group has flagged this and hasn't answered. The
substrate will track it as the thread continues.
