## Where the gains get won or lost

Smaller than the technical threads but sharper for it, the people-and-team
thread is about the layer where every individual AI gain either compounds or
evaporates: other humans. The corpus's recurring, uncomfortable finding —
the model was never the hard part; the team was.

## The arc

- **2024 — collaboration as opportunity.** Early framing is optimistic:
  diverse skill sets, future collaborations, the value of mixing people who
  know different things. AI is a reason to work together more.
- **2025 H1 — partnerships and load.** The reality of running several
  projects and partnerships at once. Funding arrives; so does the question of
  how to balance commitments without dropping the people involved.
- **2025 H2 — onboarding and context-sharing.** The concrete pain surfaces:
  bringing a new team member up to speed, and the difficulty of *sharing
  context across different AI tools.* Everyone's accumulated their own corpus
  in their own tool; getting a team to share that context is genuinely hard.
- **Late 2025 — the systemic mirror.** Ying's survey result lands like a
  cold shower: Canada ranks 44th of 47 countries in AI readiness, attributed
  to cultural and institutional factors, not capability. The team problem
  scales up to a national one.
- **2026 — teams got harder.** Jason's blunt question: *is working in teams
  harder now than before the shift?* He struggles to find a logical division
  of labor when one person plus agents can do what a team used to. The unit
  of work changed faster than the unit of organization.

## The core tension

The corpus keeps returning to a contradiction it never fully resolves:

**AI makes the individual more capable and the team more confusing.** When a
single person with a swarm can do a team's worth of output, the old logic of
dividing work breaks. Who does what, when the "what" is increasingly "direct
agents"? The collaboration that 2024 celebrated becomes, by 2026, a
coordination problem nobody has a clean answer for.

This is the social face of *atomic-to-architecture.* If everyone is pushed up
into directing systems, teams stop being assemblies of task-doers and have to
become something else — and the corpus is honest that it doesn't yet know
what.

## What stayed true

1. **Context-sharing is the team bottleneck.** Individuals build private
   corpora; teams need shared ones. The tooling for "my context" raced ahead
   of the tooling for "our context."
2. **Readiness is cultural, not technical.** The 44/47 finding: what holds
   groups back is rarely the tools. It's institutions, incentives, and
   willingness to change how people work together.
3. **The division of labor is unsolved.** When one person can do a team's
   work, "what is everyone else for" becomes a real, unanswered question.

## Open question

If individual capability keeps rising and team coordination keeps getting
harder, the corpus's own pattern — a shared, structured substrate everyone
reads and writes — is the obvious candidate for "our context." But whether a
shared corpus can actually replace the lost logic of divided labor, or just
document its absence, is the question the people-and-team thread leaves open.
