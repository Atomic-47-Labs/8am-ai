## The shape of the corpus

Every Wednesday morning since August 2025 a small group has been meeting at 8am to talk
about what's actually shifting in their work as the models get sharper. Seventeen
sessions later, the substrate has settled into thirteen recurring themes and roughly a
hundred and seventy distinct ideas. None of this was planned upfront. It accreted.

What follows isn't a transcript or a roadmap. It's the first attempt to look at the
corpus as a single thing and ask: what does the group keep coming back to?

## The five threads that keep returning

### 1. Context is the lever, not the model

The biggest single cluster (27 ideas across most weeks) is the realization that the
model itself is increasingly a commodity. What separates a useful interaction from a
useless one is whether the user can hand the model a structured, current corpus of
context — and whether the user understands what they're trying to do well enough to
recognize when the model is wrong. The group's working answer has been wiki-style
substrates, markdown corpora, and the "project state" pattern: a structured map that
both human and LLM can read.

### 2. Orchestration replaces atomic work

A close second (19 ideas) is the shift from "doing tasks" to "running systems."
Several members have rebuilt their personal workflows around the model as an
autonomous actor — triggered by commits, emails, cron jobs — and have stopped thinking
of themselves as the doer. The interesting consequence: the meetings themselves are
changing. Status updates feel obsolete; substantive thematic discussion feels scarce.

### 3. Signal harvesting as a primitive

Across 25 ideas the group keeps reinventing the same primitive: an AI skill that
reads across Slack, Gmail, Notion, repos, and surfaces "the glue" — the connective
tissue that no single human has time to track. The 8am-AI facility itself is now an
instance of that primitive. So is project-state. So is work-state. The pattern is
clearly real; the open question is whether it generalizes outside Atomic-shaped teams.

### 4. Education has a foundational-knowledge problem

A persistent thread (16 ideas, mostly led by Ying) is that students arriving with AI
in their pocket are skipping the foundational layer of any discipline. They produce
acceptable-looking output without the capacity to evaluate it. The group's working
hypothesis, suggested by Juan, is to raise problem complexity until AI alone can't
clear the bar — forcing students to use it as a learning aid rather than a substitute.

### 5. The LLM as artificial simulant

The freshest rising thread (5 ideas, recent) is the recognition that LLMs "know"
what they are from a manifest, not from experience. They don't have a privileged view
into their own errors. This has practical consequences for trust, hiring, and the
question of who or what is on the other end of an interview.

## What this corpus is, what it isn't

It isn't a strategy document. None of the threads above were anyone's agenda. They
emerged from the harvest. The reason the substrate is interesting is precisely that
it surfaces what the group keeps returning to *despite* never deciding to.

Two more threads — the future of human-LLM intersection (Neuralink, Ozempic-as-norm,
post-screen culture) and the puzzle of hiring/authentication when applicants can run
an LLM as a co-pilot during interview — are early but rising. We'll track them.

## What ships next

This post is the first in a series. Two more drafts are in this batch — one on each
of the rising themes. The remaining 167 ideas are queued; the facility will work
through them at the rate of roughly one published artifact per week, light track or
heavy. The book isn't being written. It's being assembled out of what's already been
said.
