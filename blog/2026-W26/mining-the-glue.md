Some threads describe a tool. This one describes a primitive the group kept reinventing until it became the way the group itself works: read across everything that's been said, surface the connective tissue no single human has time to track.

This blog is an instance of it. The corpus you're reading was harvested, mined, and graded by the exact pattern the group spent two years circling.

---

## the arc

2024: value out of accumulation. Framed commercially — AI product development, monetization, investment strategy. The idea that there's value latent in data you already have, if you can process it.

2025: intelligence gathering. The framing sharpens. Data anchoring — grounding model output against real project data. The realization that conversations themselves are a dataset. By mid-year the group treats its own meeting history as a source to mine, not an archive.

2025, second half: the systems question. Canadian funding, IP retention, standardization. How do you keep the value you harvest? The thread widens from "extract signal" to "own the substrate the signal lives in."

2026: quantizing intelligence, and the primitive named. The most ambitious framing arrives — standardization and quantizing intelligence, treating intelligence as something you can structure, measure, and move. And then, plainly: AI as a collaborative partner and signal harvesting. The primitive stated as a method.

---

## the four layers

The clearest artifact the thread produced is the project-state four-layer model. It's what makes harvesting repeatable instead of ad hoc.

Agents read across surfaces — Slack, email, repos, transcripts — and surface candidate signals.

Schema turns raw mentions into structured, comparable records, so signals from different weeks line up.

Data is the durable corpus the agents read and write. The thing that compounds.

Time turns a pile of records into a trajectory. One page is a note. One page across two years is a theme.

Harvesting only becomes intelligence when all four are present. Agents without schema give you noise. Schema without time gives you a snapshot. Put them together and the substrate starts telling you things no one in the room had noticed.

---

"Signal harvesting" sounds like a feature. In the corpus it's closer to an epistemology. The valuable knowledge in a group isn't in anyone's head. It's distributed across everything the group has said and done, and it stays invisible until something reads the whole record at once.

That's why the thread ends up being about the group itself. The same pattern that mines a company's Slack for synergies mines two years of Wednesday mornings for themes. Build the harvester once and you can point it at any sufficiently large record, including your own.

A harvester surfaces what its schema is shaped to see. The corpus has built the harvester. The next question is what its own schema is blind to.
