## The thread that kept changing its mind about money

The business thread is the corpus's most *commercial* — 61 ideas about what
to build, how to charge, and where the value actually sits. It's also the
thread that revised its own answer most. What looked like the business in
2024 was nearly the opposite of what looked like the business by 2026.

## The arc

- **2024 — products and projects.** Early on it's concrete: real-estate AI
  applications, project collaboration, the spreadsheet-as-product. The mental
  model is classic — build a thing, sell the thing.
- **2025 H1 — "AI as a commodity."** The pivotal reframe. If the capability
  is a commodity — if everyone can call the same models — then the model
  isn't the moat and the *product* isn't either. The group starts asking what
  is actually scarce.
- **2025 H2 — agents, context, deployment.** The answer starts forming around
  *process*: agent design, context engineering, the work of deploying and
  operating rather than just shipping. Value moves from the artifact to the
  system that produces artifacts.
- **2026 — the race to market access.** The conclusion, stated flatly: the
  *winning part is access to the market.* When anyone can build the product
  cheaply, the scarce thing is distribution — getting in front of the people
  with the problem. Pricing follows: value-based, percentage-of-revenue,
  low-friction — not seats, not licenses.

## The reframe in one line

**From "AI as a product" to "AI as a process."** It's the business thread's
version of the same move every other thread makes — atomic to architecture,
applied here to *what you sell.* You don't sell the model's output; you sell
the repeatable process that reliably produces good output in a domain, plus
the access to put it where it's needed.

This reframes the strategy advice scattered through the corpus:

- **Market access beats capability.** Two years of evidence: the build got
  cheap, so the edge relocated to distribution and trust.
- **Charge for value, not seats.** SaaS-seat pricing collapses when the
  marginal cost of the product approaches zero. Percentage-of-value aligns
  incentives and lowers the friction to "yes."
- **Be close to the problem.** The repeated counsel: get as near as possible
  to the people who actually have the pain. The domain expert who can quantify
  the ROI at the outset is worth more than the cleverest generic tool.

## The "strategic state" turn

By 2026 the thread folds back into the facility's own pattern. "Strategic
state and customer profiles" — the same structured-substrate idea applied to
the business itself: keep a living, queryable model of customers, decisions,
and pipeline, so the *business* gets the same memory and signal-harvesting the
projects do. The method the group built to mine its meetings becomes the
method it uses to run its commerce.

## What stayed true

1. **The product isn't the moat; the process and the access are.** The corpus
   re-derived this from several directions over two years.
2. **Cheap build → expensive distribution.** Every drop in build cost pushed
   value toward getting in front of the right buyer.
3. **Sell the repeatable, not the one-off.** The durable business is the
   process that reliably produces the outcome — and can be priced on the value
   of that outcome.

## Open question

If market access is the scarce thing, the next question is whether the same
AI that commoditized the product also commoditizes *distribution* — agents
that find and reach buyers at zero marginal cost. The corpus sees the race
to access clearly; it hasn't yet asked what happens when access gets cheap
too.
